Studio Statement

My practice spans painting, drawing, and sculpture—three disciplines that share a common foundation in form, gesture, and material presence. Each medium offers distinct possibilities: painting allows for layered color and atmospheric depth, drawing provides immediacy and linear precision, while sculpture engages physical space and viewer perspective. These aren't separate pursuits but interconnected explorations of the same fundamental questions about composition, balance, and visual language.

The relationship between original works and editions is central to how I think about accessibility and artistic intention. Original paintings and sculptures exist as singular objects, carrying the physical traces of their making—brushwork, surface texture, the weight of materials. Editions, whether prints or posters, translate these works into a different register: they prioritize clarity of image and democratic distribution over material uniqueness. Both modes matter. The original preserves the artifact; the edition extends the conversation.

My approach to free drawing websites and digital tools has evolved alongside traditional methods. While the core work remains rooted in physical media, I've found value in platforms like ibis paint x for preliminary sketches and compositional studies. These tools offer flexibility for experimentation without material commitment, though they complement rather than replace the tactile experience of working directly with paint, graphite, or clay. The studio remains a place where ideas move fluidly between analog and digital processes, each informing the other.

Materials

Oil and acrylic on canvas or panel, graphite and charcoal on paper, bronze and mixed-media assemblage. Choice of material follows conceptual need rather than habit.

Themes

Recurring investigations into spatial ambiguity, the tension between figuration and abstraction, and the interplay of organic and geometric forms. Work often references architectural elements and natural systems.

Working Modes

Studio practice alternates between sustained series development and one-off explorations. Commissions and gallery exhibitions run parallel to personal research projects and smaller-scale experimental work.

Method & Materials

Each piece begins as a series of sketches exploring composition, proportion, and gesture. Once the concept is resolved, I move to the final substrate—canvas for paintings, archival paper for drawings, or fabricated materials for sculpture. The transition from study to finished work is deliberate: I refine line weight, adjust color relationships, and test structural integrity before committing to the final execution.

For sculpture, surfaces are finished by hand—sanded, sealed, or patinated depending on the material. Metal receives careful oxidation or polishing; wood is treated with oils or wax to preserve grain and texture. Each finishing technique is chosen to enhance the inherent qualities of the material while ensuring longevity and stability in varied environments.

All works are photographed under calibrated daylight-balanced lighting to ensure accurate color reproduction in documentation. Archival records include material specifications, dimensions, and edition details for provenance and future conservation reference.
